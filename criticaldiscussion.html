Ethical Discussions of Generative AI

The use of generative AI is inherently complex and subject to debate because of its wide-ranging social, ethical, and political implications. Its capabilities to produce realistic text, images, audio, and video content have transformed industries but also introduced significant risks that amplify existing societal issues. One major concern is bias and fairness. Since AI models are trained on large datasets that often contain societal prejudices, their outputs can reinforce stereotypes, discrimination, and marginalization of minorities. This can lead to unfair treatment in areas like hiring, law enforcement, or media representation, raising questions about how to ensure equitable AI deployment

  • Misinformation - Misinformation and malicious use are also central challenges. Generative AI can create deepfakes, fake news, and convincing disinformation at scale, complicating efforts to maintain truth and trust in public discourse. For example, AI-generated audio or videos can be used to impersonate political figures, influence elections, or incite social unrest. These risks are especially problematic when content is difficult to detect or distinguish from authentic material.

  • Privacy - Privacy is another critical issue. Large models often rely on vast datasets that may include personal information, raising concerns about data leakage and surveillance. The potential for AI to inadvertently leak private data or be used for invasive monitoring heightens the need for strict privacy safeguards.

  • Social Implications - The social implications extend to political stability and societal trust. AI's ability to manipulate opinions, amplify divisions, and spread false content threatens democratic processes. For instance, during elections, AI-generated content can be used to sway voters, undermine confidence in institutions, or dismiss genuine scandals as fabricated.

Different ethical lenses shape how these impacts are viewed. From a deontological perspective, issues like bias, deception, and privacy violations are inherently wrong, emphasizing the need for fairness, transparency, and accountability. In contrast, consequentialist views might focus on the overall societal harm or benefit, considering whether AI’s positive uses, such as enhancing creativity or democratizing information, outweigh its risks. A virtue ethics lens would stress the importance of cultivating responsible development practices and moral character among AI creators and users.

Depending on the ethical framework, the social impacts change in emphasis. For example, a strict deontologist might argue for prohibiting certain AI applications that violate rights, while a utilitarian might advocate for deploying AI more broadly if it leads to greater societal good despite some risks. Ethical pluralism suggests that addressing these issues requires balancing multiple perspectives, including fairness, privacy, safety, and societal well-being.

The use of generative AI in hiring carries significant social implications, particularly in fairness, transparency, and accountability. While this technology improves efficiency and reduces workload, it does also raise concerns about bias and discrimination. Because AI systems are trained on historical data, they can reflect existing societal inequalities. As a result, hiring decisions may even become unfair even when there is no intention to discriminate.

From an utilitarian point of view, using generative AI in hiring is considered ethical if it leads to the greatest over benefit. AI can truly make the process faster, further reduce hiring time, and help companies handle more applications, which can benefit both employers and job seekers.

However, the downsides also do matter. If AI unfairly rejects qualified candidates or creates barriers for certain groups, the harm may outweigh the benefits. Further research has shown that biased AI can affect hiring decisions in ways that disadvantage some populations. From this perspective, generative AI is only ethical if its benefits clearly outweigh any harm it may cause.

Reccomendations

As the implications of generative AI vary from bias and fairness being particular concerns, there are final recommendations for its implementation. Specifically, applying careful consideration on whether the organization would clearly benefit from its use in order to meet desired objectives. Plus, implementing safeguards concerning data leakage are essential, namely by following strict accordance to regulation and inserting user input protection, as malicious users attempt to deceive generative AI models.
