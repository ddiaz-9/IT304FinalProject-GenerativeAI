Applications of Generative AI in the Real World

AI in Hiring Processes

Generative AI in hiring has become increasingly common as organizations search for efficient ways to manage extensive numbers of applicants. One substantial application is resume screening/candidate matching. Generative AI systems have the capability to analyze resumes, summarize applicant qualifications, and match candidates to specific job descriptions based on the skills and experience their resumes provide. Many companies now depend on automated tools to screen candidates due to the high number of applications, with studies showing that a significant portion of major organizations use some form of AI in their hiring processes.

This application proposes important social challenges. Hiring decisions directly impact an individual’s career and future opportunities. The challenge with this technology is that it learns from past data. If that data includes bias, the AI can end up repeating those same patterns. For example, a few studies have revealed that AI hiring tools have the possibility to reflect racial or gender bias when it comes to ranking candidates. This poses a serious issue for it makes it harder to ensure that the process is truly fair even if it seems objective.

Another crucial application is the use of AI-powered interview tools and chatbots. Companies use generative AI to create interview questions, run initial screenings, and communicate with applicants. These tools can be efficient in speeding things up and making it easier to handle substantial numbers of applying candidates. In many cases, AI also can help assist recruiters in reviewing applications or even sorting them before a person takes a closer look.

Even with these benefits, there are still many concerns. Applicants often are not aware of when AI is being used or how their answers are being observed. This can make the process feel unclear and less trustworthy. On top of that, AI can misunderstand responses or even overlook individual differences, which can lead to unfair results. Government agencies like the Equal Opportunity Commission (EEOC) have also warned that these tools can lead to bigger discriminatory practices even if it's not intentional.

AI in Education

Generative AI in education has grown quickly as schools are looking for improved methods to support the learning of students. An example of this is AI-powered tutoring tools, such as Khan Academy's Khanmigo. Khanmigo is an AI-powered personal tutor powered by GPT-4 and integrated directly into the Khan Academy platform. Instead of providing students answers or full explanations, it asks them guiding questions to help them think through problems on their own so that they can truly understand the material. This approach is based on the Socratic method, which is a teaching technique that encourages deeper learning through dialogue and questioning rather than direct instruction. Since its launch in 2023, Khanmigo has grown from 68,000 users to over 700,000 users in 2025.

An additional use in education is through AI tools designed for teachers. Teachers can use generative AI to create lesson plans, quiz questions, guides/rubrics, and track students' progress. For example, a high school English teacher using Khanmigo reported that creating a rubric that would normally take about an hour was completed in under 15 minutes. This is helpful because it allows teachers to spend less time on administrative tasks and more time supporting students. External platforms such as Duolingo and Quizlet have also integrated generative AI features to help students practice language skills and study material in more personalized ways.

However, there are challenges that come with using generative AI in the classroom. First, education decisions directly impact a student's academic growth, confidence, and future opportunities. A major concern with using AI tutoring tools is that they can make mistakes. Early testing of Khanmigo showed that it sometimes gave incorrect answers to basic math problems and sometimes accepted wrong answers as correct. When students trust an AI tool and receive inaccurate information without realizing it, the impact on their learning can be more damaging than it is helping.

Second, relying too much on AI tools can lead to students developing weaker critical thinking skills over time. If students regularly use AI to walk them through every problem rather than working through challenges on their own, they may struggle to think independently when they can't use an AI. Education is not just about getting the right answer, it is about building the skills to solve problems, which requires practice and productive struggle that can be hindered by over relying on AI.
